In-Store Returns Customer & Associate Experience

Crafted a 3–5 year, end-to-end strategic vision for In-Store Returns — consolidating 3 disparate return systems into one unified experience for associates while introducing new ways for customers to return items. This vision is still being executed on by other teams today.

Role
Staff UX Designer
Duration
Jan 2024 – Feb 2025
Status
Execution in progress
01The ProblemSYS.ANALYSIS

Associates processing in-store returns were forced to navigate three separate systems. A legacy platform originally built for special orders handled one type of return. As order volumes grew and new systems were introduced, the legacy platform couldn't keep up — but it was never fully replaced. The result: associates had to diagnose every return that came in, interrogate the customer about the order's origin, and then figure out which of three systems could actually process it.

If they guessed wrong, they'd hit a dead end and have to start the entire process over in a different system. This wasn't an edge case — it was the daily reality for 40,000 service desk associates across 2,335 stores.

On top of the system fragmentation, the newer return system was missing business-critical features from the legacy platform it was meant to replace. And RTV (Return to Vendor) processing — which directly determines whether the company recovers costs on returned goods — was riddled with inaccuracies. Wrong RTV data doesn't just lose money on individual transactions; it gives vendors leverage in future negotiations.

The teams responsible for these systems had been working in silos for years, often building duplicative features or shipping work that directly conflicted with other teams' goals. There was no shared vision for what the returns experience should become.

02Discovery & ResearchSYS.RECON

Research wasn't a phase — it was a cadence. We were in stores 3–4 times a month, shadowing associates at the service desk, interviewing them between transactions, and watching how they actually handled returns in real time. We paired this with customer interviews, stakeholder workshops, and detailed journey mapping to build a full picture of the current state across both the associate and customer experience.

Methods

  • In-store shadowing (3–4x/month)
  • Associate interviews
  • Customer interviews
  • Stakeholder workshops
  • Journey mapping

Key Questions

  • Where do associates lose time diagnosing returns?
  • What forces system-switching mid-transaction?
  • How do experienced vs. new associates cope?
  • Where does the customer experience break down?

03The OperatorsSYS.PROFILE

Service desk associates are the ultimate multitaskers. Returns are just one of many responsibilities competing for their attention at any given moment — alongside order pickups, order management, customer service, locating products, checkout, and a phone that never stops ringing. They need tools that let them move fast and pivot instantly, not systems that force them to investigate before they can act.

Experienced Associate

The Veteran

Service Desk Associate — 3+ years
  • Developed workarounds and tribal knowledge to navigate legacy systems
  • Knew which system to use based on gut instinct and pattern recognition
  • Could compensate for system gaps but shouldn't have had to
  • Often became the informal trainer for newer associates
New Associate

The New Hire

Service Desk Associate — under 6 months
  • Exposed to full complexity of three systems with no safety net
  • No tribal knowledge to fall back on — onboarding was a nightmare
  • Frequent dead ends and restarts led to frustration and longer transaction times
  • Customers felt the pain of their learning curve directly
04Strategic VisionSYS.STRATEGY

The vision was structured as a 3–5 year phased roadmap with two parallel swimlanes: the associate experience and the customer experience. We had to start with the associate side — building a single, consolidated returns system — before we could unlock the customer-facing innovations that depended on it.

Getting alignment was the hardest part. These teams had worked in silos for years. Through a series of stakeholder workshops, we got cross-team buy-in on both the customer and associate vision, then worked backwards into a "North Star Phased Approach" that answered the question: how do we get there?

The biggest battle was securing continued investment. The new system had been in development for 5 years and still wasn't at feature parity with the legacy platform. Leadership was close to scrapping the entire effort — keeping the disparate systems on legacy code. By reframing the conversation around customer and associate impact, and the competitive risk of falling further behind as a retailer, we shifted sentiment and drove a 3x increase in funding the following year to bring three product teams onto the effort.

05Design & IterationSYS.BUILD

I was the original designer for the single consolidated returns system and led the concepting of future enhancements including product search — a feature that let associates find the right product when a customer didn't have a receipt. Before this, associates had to jump to a separate system and hunt for the product, often selecting the wrong one. Product search eliminated the need to leave the returns flow entirely.

The consolidation fundamentally simplified the associate's workflow. Instead of diagnosing order origins across three systems and hitting dead ends, every return now starts and finishes in one place.

The vision extended well beyond the service desk. We designed two distinct return journeys — each mapped as dual-swimlane flows showing how the customer and associate experiences connect at every touchpoint.

Generic In-Store Return: The standard path. A customer walks in with an item, the associate identifies them through a loyalty program, looks up their purchase history, inspects the item, and processes the refund — all within a single system. Customer preferences like refund method are recognized automatically. No card present, no physical receipt required.

Mobile In-Store Return: The innovation layer. For customers returning oversized or bulky items — like cases of hardwood flooring — we designed a curbside return experience. The customer initiates the return online, parks in a designated space, and notifies the store they've arrived. The associate receives a notification, reviews the return details, brings a flatbed cart to the car, scans a QR code to start the process, and handles the entire return without the customer ever stepping inside the store.

This wasn't just a convenience play. It eliminated the physical burden of customers hauling heavy products through the store, reduced foot traffic at the service desk, and gave associates advance context on what was being returned before the customer even arrived.

06Outcome & ImpactSYS.RESULTS

The consolidated system has shipped and is live across all stores. Feature parity with the legacy platform has been achieved. The only remaining piece of the vision still in progress is the full "Start Online Return In Store" customer experience. Everything else — from the single associate system to product search to RTV improvements — is in production today.

The vision outlived my time on the project because it was built on durable foundations: executive buy-in earned through clear artifacts, cross-team alignment forged in stakeholder workshops, and a phased roadmap that let leadership see progress come to fruition in real time.
